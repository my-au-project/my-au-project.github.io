Builder Tried to Take Land Rebate

14 October 2025 · Contracts

Key takeaways

  • Land and build contracts should be treated as separate agreements.
  • Read every document carefully before signing, even if it seems minor.
  • If something feels unclear or risky, pause and ask for clarification.
  • Get opinions from your broker, developer, or legal adviser if needed.
  • You are not obligated to sign documents that are unclear or unnecessary.

Our land contract included a buyer's rebate of $10,000 AUD (inclusive of GST), which was to be deducted from the total land price upon settlement. This rebate was clearly documented in our land contract with the developer.

At some point during pre-construction, our builder requested that the rebate be paid to them directly by the developer and reflected as an itemised credit in our building contract. This immediately raised concerns for us.

Why this didn't make sense to us

The land and build contracts are separate agreements. The land contract is strictly between us and the land developer, and the building contract is between us and the builder.

We were also the only party required to sign this document, the land developer was not involved at all. This made the arrangement feel one-sided and unclear.

What concerned us most was a clause stating:

“Should the developer not make any payment of the invoice within 60 days, Celebration Homes will raise a variation to the contract charging the client the value of the rebate.”

Our interpretation was that if the developer refused to pay the builder, the responsibility would fall back on us, even though we had no control over the developer's actions.

This conflicted with our land contract, which already stated that the rebate would be deducted directly from the land settlement price.

Getting clarification

To understand what was going on, we emailed:

  • The sales consultant, asking for a clear explanation
  • Our mortgage broker, for an independent opinion
  • The land developer, to check whether this arrangement was acceptable

The sales consultant replied that:

“It won't be applied on your building contract. Celebration Homes more want to have record that you'll be receiving the rebate from the developer.”

However, the document itself explicitly stated that it would be applied to the building contract, something our mortgage broker also highlighted. This inconsistency made us even more uncomfortable.

Our decision

After considering all feedback, we decided not to proceed with signing the document. We responded with the following:

“Please treat the build and land contract separately. The rebate will be applied to the land settlement. We will not sign this document.”

The builder cancelled the document, and we were not required to sign it.

Final thoughts

This experience reinforced how important it is for buyers to carefully read every document they are asked to sign, even if it appears administrative or routine.

Some documents are not mandatory, and if something feels unclear or risky, it's okay to pause, seek advice, or decline. Legal advice is also an option if you're unsure.

Taking the time to understand what you're agreeing to can prevent much bigger issues later on.
